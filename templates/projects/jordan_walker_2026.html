From “Bust” to MLB Superstar: Jordan Walker Is Finally the Player We All Hoped For

The former fourth-ranked prospect in baseball has turned three years of struggle into the breakthrough season everyone expected from him — and the numbers back up every bit of it.

The former fourth-ranked prospect in all of baseball was supposed to be a star the moment he arrived. Three years and three negative-WAR seasons later, some had written him off. Now, in 2026, Jordan Walker is finally becoming exactly what everyone saw in him — and doing it in a way that looks permanent, not lucky.

With the Saint Louis Cardinals this year, Walker has been nothing but outstanding. He is putting up All-Star numbers with a slash line of .292/.346/.545 — an OPS of .891. In 71 games played, he already has a WAR of 3.4, and it is only June 18th. That wins above replacement is better than all three of his previous years in the big leagues combined.

But here is the real question. What happened this offseason that made Jordan Walker the player he is today? What changed?

The Background

Coming into his professional career, Jordan Walker was drafted in the first round of the 2020 draft by the St. Louis Cardinals. He played exceptional throughout the minors, earning many awards and playing in the 2022 All-Star Futures Game. This Georgia product was the fourth-ranked prospect in Major League Baseball coming into the 2023 season.

And the first three years of his career were — let's just say — not what we thought they were gonna be.

His first season, he hit solid — a .276 batting average, a .342 on-base percentage, and a .445 slugging percentage. But he still posted a negative WAR as a rookie. And in 2024 and 2025, that carried into back-to-back seasons also in the negatives in terms of wins above replacement. In those three years combined, Jordan Walker racked up 280 strikeouts against just 76 walks. He was chasing pitches out of the zone at a 34.1% rate. He was swinging at strikes he should have hit and taking balls he should have laid off. Statistically, he was one of the worst hitters in professional baseball. He was even labeled a bust by many baseball fans. And he heard every word of it.

What Changed

So what actually changed? It was not one thing. It was everything — at the same time.

This past offseason, Jordan Walker made a trip to Driveline Baseball — one of the most respected player development facilities in the country — and went to work on a flaw that had been quietly killing his production for three years. His back hip was collapsing on his swing, preventing him from getting under the ball and elevating it. For a guy with his kind of raw power, hitting grounders to shortstop is a death sentence. They fixed it.

He also walked into 2026 with a new hitting coach. Cardinals hitting coach Brant Brown, along with Casey Chenoweth and Brandon Allen, went back to the film and compared 2023 to 2024. What they found was that Walker's hands were separating from his body too early and his lower half balance was completely off. They rebuilt it.

"There's something he's anchored to now. When it comes to having success, you're going to get off track from time to time. But now we know what we need to get back to."

— Oliver Marmol, St. Louis Cardinals Manager

The numbers prove that every single adjustment worked.

The Mechanics

His average attack angle — the upward tilt of his bat at the moment of contact — went from 5 degrees in 2023 all the way to 8 degrees in 2026. That may sound small. It is not. That change alone flipped his fly ball rate from 22% to 40%. He went from one of the most ground ball-heavy hitters in baseball to one of the least — almost overnight.

The Discipline

His chase rate — the percentage of pitches outside the strike zone that he swings at — dropped from 34.1% in 2025 all the way down to 23.1% in 2026. His in-zone swing rate went up from 66.9% to 75%. He is swinging at the right pitches now and laying off the ones designed to get him out. His first pitch swing rate went from 36.5% last year to 44.9% this year — meaning he is being more aggressive when pitchers come after him early, and he is doing damage with it.

The Power

And the exit velocity? His average exit velocity is up nearly 5 miles per hour from where it was. He is sitting at 94.7 miles per hour on average, with a hard-hit rate of 52.7% and a barrel rate of 14.1. Against left-handed pitchers this year, his OPS is .953. Against right-handers, it is .855. He does not have a weakness right now.

The Verdict

So is this just a hot streak? Or did Jordan Walker — the fourth-ranked prospect in baseball four years ago — finally figure it out? The Driveline work, the new coaching staff, the stance overhaul, the pitch discipline, the attack angle, the fly ball rate — all of it moved in the right direction at the same time. That is not luck. That is a player who put in the work, found what was broken, fixed it, and came back ready.

This year, Jordan Walker has become a complete hitter. His splits are almost identical between righty and lefty matchups. He is hitting the cover off of a baseball. And it is so fun to watch this young player at 24 years old perform and finally reach his potential.

And believe it or not — he is not done yet.

The Saint Louis Cardinals are five games out of the Milwaukee Brewers right now, pushing for that NL Central division lead. Jordan Walker is their catalyst. He is their offensive firepower. He is the reason this team is legitimate.

Can the Cardinals win the division — or at least grab a Wild Card spot to make the playoffs this year? Who knows. But if they do, you better believe Jordan Walker is going to be a main reason they are there.
