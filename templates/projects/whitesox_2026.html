Strategy — Analysis 09

Path to World Series Contention

The 2026 White Sox are entering year three of their rebuild with genuine upside. Vargas, Montgomery, Meidroth, and Antonacci are all legitimate contributors under team control. The win curve is pointing up. Here is the blueprint to close the remaining gap.

Priority #1 Do This Before Anything Else

Extend Munetaka Murakami

Murakami is the best hitter on this roster — and it is not close. Twenty-two home runs in 60 games, a .378 OBP, an OPS north of .946 from the first base position, and an Rbat+ of 157 puts him in the MVP conversation. He is 26 years old, under contract through 2027 on a 2-year, $34M deal ($16.5M in 2026, $17.5M in 2027) — already paying him above the league's standard for his service class, but still a significant discount relative to his production. After 2027 he is a free agent, and the open market will price a 28-year-old MVP-caliber first baseman accordingly. The White Sox need to initiate extension talks now. A five-year extension at $20–22M AAV buying out his free-agent years would be the most important contract in franchise history. Every month this drags out, the ask goes up. There is no Plan B. He is the anchor.

Offseason Roster Decisions

Let Walk: Benintendi & Jordan Hicks

Andrew Benintendi's deal was the largest contract in White Sox franchise history at the time it was signed — five years, $75M ($15M AAV). The return: -0.8 WAR through 63 games, a .274 OBP, and a lineup spot better allocated elsewhere. The organizational milestone was the signing itself. The production has not justified renewal. Do not pick up any option or extension.

Jordan Hicks (4 yr/$44M, $12.5M in 2026) expires after 2027 as well. He's a power arm but a $12.5M closer on a team spending $88M total is a luxury this roster doesn't need locked in. Let the contract expire and use that money toward a starting pitcher or controlled outfielder. The payroll freed from both departures — roughly $29–30M — is the foundation of the 2027 offseason.

Retain Through Arbitration

Vargas, Antonacci, Colson Montgomery, Meidroth — all four should be retained through arbitration. The aggregate arb cost across these four will climb toward $14–18M by 2027 (Vargas alone could reach $5–6M at Arb-2), but every dollar is justified. This group combined for over 7.2 WAR through 63 games. A viable contender does not let that core walk to save money on arbitration.

These are not fringe players being paid above market — they are cheap, controlled contributors who will anchor the lineup through at least 2028.

The Ace Question — The Biggest Decision This Franchise Will Make

The White Sox rotation lacks a true front-line starter. The team can win 85–87 games with the current core, but 90+ games — the kind that locks up a Wild Card and keeps the Division in play — requires an ace at the top of the rotation. The 2027 free agent class offers three legitimate targets:

Shane Bieber — Returns from injury in 2026. If healthy, one of the most efficient starters in baseball: command-first profile, sub-3.00 ERA potential, and a demonstrated Cy Young ceiling. Lower floor than a power arm, but a known commodity who controls the zone at an elite level. A lower-risk option in the $22–26M AAV range.

Tarik Skubal — The best left-hander in baseball, period. Elite strikeout and walk rates, top-5 FIP in the American League, and the kind of durability and stuff combination teams pay generational money to secure. If he reaches free agency in 2027, he will cost $28–32M AAV — the highest pitcher commitment in White Sox history by a wide margin.

Brandon Woodruff — Perennial injury risk, but a genuine top-10 arm when healthy. A potential $20–24M AAV alternative if Reinsdorf will not go past the Grandal line. The health track record is the only legitimate concern; the talent is unquestionable.

The Benintendi deal set the franchise record for total value. The Grandal deal set it for per-year value. Signing an ace-caliber starter would break both simultaneously. This is the inflection point.

The Outfield Problem

Option A — Brayden Montgomery (Internal)

The team's top outfield prospect. If his trajectory holds through the 2026 minor-league season, he is a candidate for a full-time role in 2027. Cost: zero dollars. Risk: asking a young player to carry a starting OF spot on a contending team before he is fully ready. The upside here is legitimate — so is the development variance.

Option B — Bring Back Randal Grichuk

Grichuk is hitting .327 / .357 / .673 with six home runs in 56 plate appearances (1.030 OPS, 189 OPS+). At 34, on a short-term deal in the $4–6M range, he is a legitimate right field option — power, experience, and comfortable in a platoon or near-everyday role. Not a franchise-level acquisition, but a smart bridge while Montgomery develops.

Option C — Trade Using Prospect Capital

The White Sox have five years of top-five draft picks banked in the system. A controlled, above-average outfielder acquired via trade is the highest-ceiling option and does not require Reinsdorf to write a free-agent check. Cost: future assets. If the 2027 window is real, this is the lever that signals the organization believes it.

Bullpen Upgrade

A World Series roster needs three legitimate high-leverage relievers. The 2026 White Sox bullpen is a mix of young arms with upside and veterans holding roster spots. One controllable, high-leverage arm should be targeted at the July 2026 trade deadline. Two bullpen spots should be addressed in the 2026–27 offseason — either via targeted free agency ($8–15M for a closer-caliber arm) or trade using pitching depth from the system. A starting rotation gets you to October; a bullpen wins in October.

2026 MLB Draft — Setting the Foundation for 2029 and Beyond

The White Sox are projected to hold the #1 overall pick in the 2026 MLB Draft. Two names are at the center of the conversation:

#1 Candidate Roch Cholowsky, SS — UCLA — Polished college shortstop with a plus hit tool, above-average arm, and advanced plate discipline rare for a 21-year-old. Lower ceiling than a premium prep arm, but a dramatically safer floor. Could be contributing at the big-league level within two to three years.

#2 Candidate Grady Emerson, RHP — Fort Worth Christian HS (Fort Worth, TX) — High school right-hander with frontline starter potential and the best pure stuff in the class. Higher variance, higher ceiling. Signability is a factor at the prep level, but if the White Sox are targeting a 2027–29 window, they do not need him to contribute immediately — they need him to be dominant in 2030–31.

Either pick adds a foundational piece to a system that is already producing at the big-league level. The farm does not need to be gutted to contend — it needs to stay loaded while the major-league window opens.

The 2027 Window Is Real

If the 2026 Chicago White Sox finish at or just above .500 — and the current trajectory is pointing in that direction — the front office will face its most consequential offseason in a decade. The core is young, controlled, and producing. Murakami is an MVP-caliber bat at a below-market salary. Vargas, Montgomery, Meidroth, and Antonacci are all under 27 and improving. The revenue model shows every win near the 88-win threshold is worth $5–6M in marginal revenue — the Gaussian curve is steep right there. Adding an ace starter and a true starting outfielder pushes this team from an 82-win club to an 89-win playoff contender. The pieces are there. The analytics support the commitment. The only remaining question is whether Jerry Reinsdorf is willing to spend like he believes it.
