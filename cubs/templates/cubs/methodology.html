Methodology

How the optimal batting order is computed from Baseball Reference data.

Step 1 — Data

Source Data

Nine CSV exports from Baseball Reference are joined into one master DataFrame at startup. The join key is the player's unique Baseball Reference ID (the Player-additional or Name-additional column). Players that appear in standard batting but not in a supplemental table retain a NaN for that table's columns rather than being dropped — so no player is excluded due to a missing supplemental file.

  • Standard Batting — slash line (BA/OBP/SLG), HR, RBI, SB, WAR, OPS+, rOBA, Rbat+
  • Advanced Batting — BAbip, ISO, EV, Hard-Hit%, LD/GB/FB%, Pull/Cent/Oppo%
  • Value Batting — Rbat, Rbaser, Rfield, oWAR, dWAR
  • Neutralized Batting — park-neutral Runs Created (RC_neut)
  • Team Baserunning — SB%, RS%, XBT%, SBO
  • Batting Ratios — SO/W, AB/HR, GO/AO, HR/FB
  • Sabermetric Batting — RC, RC/G, SecA, TotA, PwrSpd
  • Win Probability — WPA, Clutch, aLI, RE24, REW
  • Situational Hitting — vs RHP/LHP, Home/Road HR splits
Step 2 — Player Selection

Selecting the Nine Starters

The algorithm treats the top 9 players by Plate Appearances as the everyday starters. This reflects actual manager usage — the players who have accumulated the most PA are the ones pencilled in most often. No positional constraints are applied at this stage; the algorithm optimizes purely on offensive production.

Step 3 — Scoring

Batting-Spot Fitness Scores

All scoring stats are min-max normalized to [0, 1] within the nine starters so that no single statistic dominates due to scale differences. A weighted formula then rates each player's fitness for each of spots 1–6:

Spot 1 — Leadoff

OBP (35%) + SB% (25%) + Contact/K-rate (25%) + SecA (15%)
Prioritizes getting on base and stealing bases.

Spot 2 — 2-Hole

OBP (30%) + BA (20%) + SecA (25%) + SB% (25%)
On-base + contact + ability to move runners.

Spot 3 — Best Hitter

OPS+ (25%) + rOBA (25%) + RC/G (25%) + WAR (25%)
Pure overall offensive value.

Spot 4 — Cleanup

SLG (30%) + HR (30%) + ISO (20%) + RBI (20%)
Power and RBI production first.

Spot 5 — Secondary Power

SLG (25%) + HR (20%) + RBI (30%) + RC/G (25%)
Protects the cleanup hitter.

Spot 6 — Bottom Anchor

OPS (40%) + RBI (30%) + BA (30%)
Best overall hitter in the bottom third.

Spots 7–9 are filled with the three unassigned players sorted by OPS descending — ensuring the best of the "bottom of the order" players still appear higher in the bottom third.

Step 4 — Optimization

Finding the Best Overall Lineup

A 9×6 score matrix is built — each row is a player, each column is a batting spot (1–6). The model then solves an assignment problem: rather than greedily picking the highest-scoring player for spot 1, then spot 2, and so on (which can box out better fits for later spots), it considers every possible way to assign 9 players to 6 spots and selects the single arrangement where the combined fitness score across all six spots is highest. The result is a globally optimal lineup — not locally optimized slot by slot, but the best possible combination overall. SciPy's linear_sum_assignment solves this efficiently in milliseconds.

Step 5 — Limitations

Known Limitations

  • No platoon splits in assignment. The algorithm does not account for pitcher handedness or matchup-specific splits — it optimizes on season-aggregate stats only.
  • PA-based starter selection. Using PA as a proxy for "starter" may include players who accumulated PA via pinch-hitting rather than starting every game.
  • Positional constraints ignored. A lineup could theoretically place two catchers; the algorithm has no defensive validity check.
  • Small-sample noise. Early in the season, stats may not be representative of true talent; projections or multi-year averages would reduce noise.
  • Run-expectancy weights are approximations. The per-spot weights above are research-informed estimates, not derived from live Markov-chain simulation of the Cubs roster specifically.
